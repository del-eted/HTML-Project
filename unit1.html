1.1 The purpose of IT in modern business

IT is fundamental to modern businesses, as data forms the foundation of modern problem solutions and customer service methods - personal identifiable data, remotely accessed, can allow a customer service agent to remotely access a customers details in real time, and provide direct support, advice and updates (to both customer, the database of information or to a third party, EG, a telephone engineer) while speaking to the customer. This is the basis of call centers, the first point of contact for many businesses and fundamental to many business income generation models in many industries in the UK today.

The volume of data processed and the speed it is processed at would be impossible for anything but an IT solution to handle - a pure human model would be immensely expensive and operate at a fraction of the speed of an IT dependent model; this can be observed in the largest companies in the world; many of which are IT business, or even data-dependent companies, such as Google, Facebook, IBM, Microsoft and Apple. Industries are being automated by IT solutions - from more popular and familiar examples such as car production factories or modern offices and accounting solutions, to less familiar examples such as spaceflight, design - even pure traditional animation techniques are compiled digitally, and served in a digital format.

Without IT solutions, such as remote storage, data processing, data storage, remote input, accessible apps, websites and online stores a company in the modern world cannot hope to become and remain competitive, as IT solutions are a part of everyday life with the proliferation of home computers, some in disguise such as smartTVs into our homes and our reliance upon mobile technologies such as laptops and smartphones to help us organise our lives, our data and our schedules. 1.4 The risks to a digital projects

Mid-development updates to dependant libraries or OS’ can negatively impact or even break coded functions, requiring already developed functions to be re-developed. Hardware and software development costs can create limitations in workload capacity, bottle-necking development and further increasing costs.

Unexpected loss of data can occur with hardware or sudden power failures that cause unsaved work to be lost, or if off-site data storage suffers failures, or temporary outages that can disrupt workflow. With group projects multiple users editing documents can also lead to duplication or accidental editing errors without proper care or moderating. Data migration can cause a loss of customer data for large projects, or a corruption of data in smaller ones. Data security in third-party storage or remote-access storage can also be a risk during development, leading to security and data leaks that jeopardize the project or even the entire company. Simple security risks such as weak passwords and failing to lock a computer when leaving a workstation can also be a risk to security. [3]

1.7 Copyright and GDPR

GDPR stands for “General Data Protection Regulations” and is an EU guideline that came into force on May 25th 2018 relating to data storage and protection. It defines data as;

“any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person—including identifiers such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier, or, for example, factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person” [1]


It is a guideline for the storage and use of personal data, and gives the right of individuals to see the data stored about them, and even force the cessation of processing of that data.

For digital companies it also enforces that all processing of personal data must be done with the mind of minimising data - restricting the used information to the bare minimum to restrict the movement of personal data, and thereby help reduce the risks and impact of leaked or lost personal data, including the collecting of unnecessary information, for example, taking an email address for future marketing purposes. One common way of anonymising data, especially for data analysis collection (thus, not requiring it to be compliant with GDPR) is to remove any personal identifiers - IPs, names, addresses, etc, while still collecting sensitive data like date of birth, ethnic origin, income, etc.

The legislation also helps bring foreign competitors operating within the EU (which, in todays modern digital market, means globally) must also comply to EU GDPR. [4]

2.3 Discuss the strengths and weaknesses of your final project

The final product works as intended on desktop browsers, but does not function properly on mobile devices, as the images and text used do not scale adequately to the different aspect ratios and the screen resolutions. Despite using CSS code that relies on the default font settings of the browsers used to strengthen accessibility to the site, the text remains small and difficult to read on mobile devices, particularly mobile phones. Therefore a default text size would be a stronger choice for a mobile version of the site.

This reliance on the browsers code however is very helpful when viewing from customised browsers - such as those used from a more comfortably formatted home computer (in particular, my home computer uses larger font sizes so it can be used without my glasses, and the code used adjusts to this automatically without issue).

The site is also built around colour theory principles, giving each page a theme through backgrounds, using similarly grouped and formatted text with off-black and off-white colours selected to help make them easier on the eye, remove visual noise and make them less distracting for the viewer - something discussed in accessibility, but also fits well into modern design aesthetics. I also desaturated the backgrounds used to remove visual noise, bring them closer together thematically and visually, and again remove visual distractions despite the complexity of some of the background images used.

The use of parallax scrolling makes the website look modern and dynamic, and also helps to separate text into what appear to be smaller, more easily readable chunks - a visual trick often used in graphic design for infographic posters and images. This is a fairly simple coding trick, but an important front end decision.



2.4 Further improvements you could make to your project

The site could identify the browsers aspect ratio or device used to view the site, and direct to a mobile compatible version of the site using different backgrounds created in vertical aspect ratios specifically for mobile devices, and seperate side-menus rather than a top navigation bar, as is the standard for mobile versions of websites and apps, and use larger buttons for touch-screen use rather than the precision of a mouse.

I also feel that some of the background images used could be improved, given the time to find stronger images (or, for a professional or personal project, create them - to avoid infringement of copyright and also have full control over thematic and colour choices).
